The War Inside Your Head

By Kyle Aleixo

You have probably heard someone say "just get over it" when talking about pain or something that happened years ago. People tell you to move on and bury it. But what if burying it is exactly what makes it worse and what if the stuff you push down doesn't disappear but just waits?

That is the point of Kendrick Lamar's 2022 album Mr. Morale & The Big Steppers. Across 18 tracks Kendrick doesn't just rap about trauma, he shows what happens inside a person's mind when they refuse to deal with it and what it looks like when they do. Through a psychoanalytical lens, the album's narrative is driven by the conflict between three parts of the psyche, the Id, the Ego and the Superego. The journey from repression to chaos to healing matters far beyond music, because if you don't confront your inner conflict you are doomed to repeat past mistakes.

Chapter 1

Before You Press Play: The Album Cover

Before a single beat drops, the album cover shows Kendrick's thoughts in one image. It shows the Superego and Id locked in conflict while the Ego is missing.

The first thing your eyes land on is the crown of thorns pressed into Kendrick's head. He holds his daughter in one arm while his partner sits behind him nursing their son. Tucked into his waistband, hidden behind his back, is a gun. His daughter cannot see it and his family cannot see it. Only the viewer can.

The crown of thorns represents Kendrick's Superego, the voice that demands moral perfection and punishes any failure to meet it. It is not just guiding Kendrick, it is physically digging into him. The gun represents the Id, the instinctual and violent part of the mind. It has been pushed out of sight and repressed behind his back where the people he loves cannot see it, but it is still loaded and still dangerous. The Ego, the part of the mind that is supposed to mediate between guilt and impulse, is absent. With no one mediating, the Superego forces him to keep a perfect image to his family while the Id silently arms itself and the album is the result of that conflict.

Chapter 2

When the Lid Comes Off: "We Cry Together"

By track 8 the repression from the album cover collapses completely and the Id takes full control with zero input from the Ego.

"We Cry Together" is not a song in any traditional sense. It is a screaming argument between Kendrick and actress Taylour Paige with no pauses, no compromise and no reflection. They tear into each other with escalating rage, saying the worst things they can think of. The track ends not with resolution but with the two of them having sex. Nothing gets settled.

Every word in the fight is pure impulse, which is the Id clearly operating. Neither person pauses to think and neither considers consequences, because the Ego that is supposed to think rationally is absent. There is no internal voice stepping in to slow anything down. Without that mediator the argument escalates forever and every insult gets louder because nothing inside either of them is built to interrupt it. This highlights Kendrick's clear response at the start of the album is only with impulsive thought. Even with this need for the perfect image seen in the cover he can not use his ego to think rationally to fix a situation.

When the fight shifts into sex, it proves that nothing was confronted. The Id just switched from aggression to pleasure without the Ego ever stepping in, which shows that impulse can disguise itself as resolution when it is really just avoidance. The couple looks like they made up, but they did the opposite. This is why the cycle cannot break on its own. Impulse does not solve impulse, it just changes shape and the same unresolved conflict will come back in a different form the next time it is triggered.

Chapter 3

Looking in the Mirror: "Mirror"

The album's final track is where the Ego finally arrives and Kendrick makes rational thought for the first time.

After 17 songs of repression and self destruction, Kendrick arrives at "Mirror". The song itself is an ironic title for the self reflection that takes place throughout its duration. Kendrick looks back on his life like a mirror and finally finds the strength to say "I chose me I'm sorry" (Lamar).

Naming the track "Mirror" is the first sign the Ego has arrived. Every song before this aimed outward at someone else, but a mirror only points back at the person in front of it. By titling the closing track this way, Kendrick admits the whole album was about him, which is the exact move the Ego is built for, forcing him to stop projecting and start seeing himself.

The line "I chose me I'm sorry" (Lamar) shows the Ego balancing both sides instead of being controlled by them. "I chose me" lets the Id finally exist, while "I'm sorry" keeps the Superego's perfect image so that choice does not become harmful. Neither is greater, they both share the sentence, which is how a healthy Ego is supposed to work.

Why it matters

Why This Matters to You

Here is the thing about trauma. It does not need to be dramatic to be real and you do not need Kendrick Lamar's story for this to apply to you. Maybe it is anxiety you have never talked about. Maybe it is anger you do not understand. Maybe it is a pattern in your relationships that keeps repeating no matter what you do. Maybe it is something a parent passed down without either of you realizing it.

The instinct is to bury it. To push it down, numb it, distract yourself and perform like everything is fine. But Mr. Morale makes the case, across 18 tracks and one album cover, that burying it is not healing. It is just delaying the explosion.

Confronting your inner conflict is not comfortable and Kendrick makes that clear. It took him an entire double album to get to the point where he could say "I choose me" (Lamar). But the alternative, the repression and the cycles that repeat over and over is worse and it will always be worse.

The gun behind your back does not disappear just because you hide it in the back of your mind. Sooner or later you are not also going to spiral but someone close to you might also.

The only question is whether you put it down first.
